Hello, my name is Sebastian and I’m a PhD student in cognitive psychology at [Princeton University](https://cognition.princeton.edu/). I work in Tania Lombrozo’s concept and cognition lab.

Broadly, I am interested in the intersection of cognitive science, philosophy of science, and philosophy of mind. For instance, how do people intuitively compare theories that share the same evidence but differ in their theoretical virtues (e.g., simplicity or ad-hocness)? What are our folk beliefs about consciousness, and how does it influence our proclivity towards dualistic thinking? What are the evolutionary origins of religious belief?

My current research focus is on a classic question in the philosophy of science: is there some advantage to being able to predict data ahead of time, as opposed to accommodating it into your theory after the fact? On the one hand, philosophers and scientists have historically thought there is something *ad hoc* about accommodation. On the other hand, why would the order in which you learn that data obtained matter to the reliability of your theory? We are trying to investigate this empirically, by looking at how people who learn using these two strategies differ.
